North America

The United States, Canada, and Mexico form a diverse region of vast landscapes, economic integration, and complex cultural dynamics spanning from Arctic tundra to tropical beaches.

Physical Geography

Major Landforms

The Rocky Mountains: Running from Alaska through Canada and the western U.S., these young fold mountains shape climate and settlement patterns. Snow-capped peaks rise above 14,000 feet, and the Continental Divide runs along their crest.

The Great Plains: The agricultural heartland stretching from Canada to Texas produces much of North America's grain. Flat to gently rolling terrain with rich prairie soils supports wheat, corn, and cattle ranching.

The Appalachians: Older, lower mountains in the east that historically limited westward expansion. Rich in coal deposits, these worn mountains supported early American industrial development.

The Canadian Shield: A vast area of exposed Precambrian rock covering much of eastern and central Canada. Rich in minerals but with thin, acidic soils that limit agriculture.

Climate Patterns

North America's climate varies dramatically from Arctic conditions in northern Canada to tropical climates in southern Mexico. Key patterns include:

  • Continental Climate: Hot summers and cold winters in the interior
  • Marine West Coast: Mild, wet conditions along the Pacific Northwest
  • Mediterranean: Dry summers, wet winters in California
  • Tropical: Year-round warmth in southern Florida and Mexico

üåæ Agricultural Regions

North America is one of the world's most productive agricultural regions, with the United States and Canada among the top food exporters globally.

üåê Global Theme: Urbanization and Diversity

North America is highly urbanized, with about 82% of the population living in metropolitan areas. Major cities like New York, Los Angeles, Toronto, Mexico City, and Houston drive economic and cultural life.

Immigration and Cultural Diversity

Immigration has made North American cities among the world's most diverse, with significant populations from every world region. The United States, Canada, and Mexico each have distinct immigration histories and policies.

Economic Integration: USMCA

The United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA, formerly NAFTA) creates one of the world's largest free trade zones. Key features include:

  • Elimination of most tariffs between the three countries
  • Integrated supply chains, especially in automotive manufacturing
  • Maquiladoras (assembly plants) along the U.S.-Mexico border
  • Energy trade, especially oil and natural gas

üîç Geographic Inquiry

The U.S.-Mexico border is one of the world's most economically integrated yet socially divided boundaries. How can geographic analysis explain both the intense economic ties (trade, supply chains) and the intense political disputes (immigration, drug trafficking, border security)?

Texas exemplifies North American geographic diversity, spanning multiple climate zones, cultural influences, and economic systems. As the second-largest state by both area and population, Texas connects the U.S. to Mexico through the world's busiest land border.

Key Geographic Features:

  • Border Region: The Rio Grande Valley supports agriculture and manufacturing
  • Gulf Coast: Petroleum refining, shipping, and hurricane vulnerability
  • Hill Country: Tourism and ranching in the Edwards Plateau
  • Panhandle: Cotton, cattle, and wind energy on the High Plains
