Latin America

From the Amazon rainforest to Andean peaks, Latin America encompasses extraordinary biodiversity and cultural richness, shaped by Indigenous heritage, colonial legacies, and persistent inequality.

Physical Geography

Major Landforms

The Andes: The world's longest continental mountain range runs 7,000 km along South America's western edge. These young, rugged mountains include some of the highest peaks in the Western Hemisphere, reaching over 22,000 feet at Aconcagua in Argentina.

The Amazon Basin: The world's largest rainforest and river system, containing 20% of Earth's freshwater runoff. The Amazon River stretches over 4,000 miles and drains an area the size of the contiguous United States.

The Pampas: Fertile grasslands of Argentina and Uruguay, important for agriculture. These temperate prairies support cattle ranching and grain production similar to the Great Plains of North America.

The Brazilian Highlands: A large plateau covering much of eastern Brazil, rich in minerals including iron ore and gold. Major cities like S√£o Paulo and Rio de Janeiro developed on this elevated terrain.

üìú Colonial Legacy

European colonization profoundly shaped Latin America's geography, demographics, and economy. The 1494 Treaty of Tordesillas divided South America between Spain and Portugal.

The Columbian Exchange

The exchange of plants, animals, and diseases between the Americas and Europe transformed both hemispheres:

  • To Europe: Potatoes, tomatoes, corn, chocolate, tobacco
  • To Americas: Horses, cattle, wheat, sugarcane, diseases
  • Population collapse: Indigenous populations declined by 90% due to European diseases

üîç Geographic Inquiry

The Amazon rainforest is often called "the lungs of the planet." If we lose this critical ecosystem to deforestation, how would the geographic impacts ripple beyond Latin America to affect global climate, Indigenous communities, and future development possibilities?

üåê Global Theme: Biodiversity and Inequality

Latin America contains extraordinary biodiversity but also some of the world's highest income inequality. The region exemplifies the tension between development and environmental protection.

Amazon Deforestation

Deforestation in the Amazon threatens global climate while development pressures intensify. Key drivers include:

  • Cattle ranching expansion
  • Soybean agriculture
  • Logging (legal and illegal)
  • Mining operations
  • Road construction

Urbanization

Latin America is one of the world's most urbanized regions, with over 80% of the population living in cities. Megacities like S√£o Paulo, Mexico City, and Buenos Aires face challenges of rapid growth, informal settlements, and infrastructure demands.

Texas shares 1,254 miles of border with Mexico, creating a unique binational region. Trade, migration, and cultural exchange flow across this boundary daily, making Texas deeply connected to Latin America.

Border Economy:

  • Maquiladoras: Manufacturing plants on the Mexican side that rely on cross-border trade
  • Ports of Entry: Laredo is the busiest land port in the Western Hemisphere
  • Energy Trade: Texas refineries process Mexican oil
  • Cultural Exchange: Tex-Mex cuisine, music, and language reflect this fusion
