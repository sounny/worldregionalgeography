Latin America: Biodiversity and Inequality

From the Amazon rainforest to Andean peaks, Latin America encompasses extraordinary biodiversity and cultural richness, shaped by Indigenous heritage, colonial legacies, and persistent inequality.

Latin America is a region of vast physical contrasts - from the world's longest continental mountain range (the Andes) to the largest tropical rainforest (the Amazon). Its history is a record of intense cultural fusion and structural inequality born from centuries of extractive colonial rule.

South America's physical geography is dominated by the Andes Mountains, which run the entire length of the western coast. This rugged spine creates sharp climatic divides, including the parched Atacama Desert and the lush Amazonian slopes.

A key concept here is Altitudinal Zonation - the way climate and agricultural potential change based on elevation rather than just latitude.

üë• Human Geography: Colonial Legacies and Favelas

Latin America's cultural landscape is a product of the "Columbian Exchange" - the massive multi-directional flow of people, plants, and diseases between Europe and the Americas. The Treaty of Tordesillas (1494) explains the regional divide between Spanish and Portuguese speakers.

Today, the region is 80% urbanized, yet faces extreme income inequality. The economy relies heavily on remittances (money sent home by migrants) and the informal sector.

A Binational Reality

Texas shares a 1,254-mile border with Mexico, creating a unique geographic "shatterbelt" where cultures and economies fuse. The Tex-Mex cultural landscape is not just about food; it's a geographic reality of shared history, migration, and trade.

Trade Corridors: The Port of Laredo is the busiest inland port in the US, serving as the primary gateway for goods moving between the integrated supply chains of North and Latin America. What happens in the Mexican economy has immediate geographic and economic ripples across the state of Texas.

Case Study

The Panama Canal: A Strategic Chokepoint

The Panama Canal is one of the world's most critical "functional regions." By slicing through the Isthmus of Panama, it connects the Atlantic and Pacific oceans, fundamentally altering global trade geography. Today, it faces a new geographic challenge: climate change. Droughts in the watershed are lowering lake levels, restricting the number of ships that can pass through this vital chokepoint.

Questions to Consider:

  • How does the "physical geography" of Panama (rainfall/forests) directly impact the "economic geography" of global trade?
  • If the canal's capacity is reduced, which world regions are most affected?
