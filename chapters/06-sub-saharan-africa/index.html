Sub-Saharan Africa

The world's youngest and fastest-growing population, extraordinary biodiversity, and rapid urbanization define this diverse region of 48 countries south of the Sahara Desert.

Physical Geography

A Continent of Plateaus

Sub-Saharan Africa is dominated by extensive plateau areas, with few major mountain ranges compared to other continents. The key features include:

  • The Great Rift Valley: A massive geological fault system stretching from Southwest Asia down through East Africa, creating deep lakes and volcanic mountains.
  • The Congo Basin: A vast, tropical rainforest basin in Central Africa, second only to the Amazon in size.
  • The Sahel: A semi-arid transition zone between the Sahara Desert to the north and the tropical savannas to the south, susceptible to desertification.

üìú Historical Context

Africa is the cradle of humanity, but its recent history has been defined by the Scramble for Africa, where European powers arbitrarily divided the continent at the Berlin Conference of 1884.

  • Colonial Borders: Boundaries were drawn without regard for ethnic or linguistic groups, leading to lasting political instability.
  • Independence: Most countries gained independence in the 1950s and 60s but faced challenges in nation-building.

üîç Geographic Inquiry

Colonial borders in Africa were drawn by European powers with little regard for ethnic, cultural, or religious boundaries. How do these arbitrary geographic boundaries continue to shape conflicts, trade patterns, and development challenges in Sub-Saharan Africa today?

üåê Global Theme: Urbanization and Development

Sub-Saharan Africa is the world's fastest-urbanizing region. While traditionally rural, millions are moving to cities like Lagos, Kinshasa, and Nairobi for economic opportunities.

The Dual Economy

African cities often feature a dual economy:

  • Formal Sector: Government, large corporations, and global trade.
  • Informal Sector: Street vending, small-scale services, and artisan work, which employs the majority of the urban poor.

Texas and Sub-Saharan Africa share strong ties in the energy sector. Houston-based companies are heavily involved in oil exploration and production in West Africa (Nigeria, Angola). Furthermore, many Texas universities host African scholars and students, fostering a knowledge exchange in agriculture, engineering, and geosciences.
