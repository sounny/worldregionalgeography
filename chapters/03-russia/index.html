Russia & Central Asia: Resources and Geopolitics

Spanning eleven time zones, Russia is the world's largest country. Together with the Central Asian republics, this region bridges Europe and Asia with vast resources, complex politics, and enduring geographic challenges.

Physical Geography

Major Physical Regions

The Russian Plain: Western Russia consists of flat to rolling terrain extending from the western borders to the Ural Mountains. This region contains the agricultural heartland, major rivers (Volga, Don), and the vast majority of Russia's population. The city of Moscow sits at the center of this plain.

The Ural Mountains: These ancient, eroded mountains form the traditional boundary between Europe and Asia. Rich in minerals including iron, copper, and precious metals, the Urals were critical to Soviet industrialization.

Siberia: The vast eastern region between the Urals and the Pacific contains three sub-regions: the West Siberian Plain (oil and gas deposits), the Central Siberian Plateau (mineral wealth), and the Russian Far East (Pacific coast). Extreme continental climate dominates, with permafrost underlying much of the territory. The taiga (boreal forest) is the world's largest forest biome.

Central Asia: The five "-stan" countries (Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan) feature arid steppes and deserts in the lowlands, with the Tian Shan and Pamir mountains along southern borders. The Aral Sea, once the world's fourth-largest lake, has shrunk dramatically due to Soviet-era irrigation projects.

Climate and Vegetation

Russia's climate reflects its vast size and continental position. Key features include:

  • Extreme continentality: Verkhoyansk in Siberia holds records for the coldest temperatures outside Antarctica
  • Permafrost: Permanently frozen ground underlies about 65% of Russian territory
  • Vegetation zones: From north to south: tundra, taiga (boreal forest), mixed forest, steppe, and desert

üìú The Soviet Legacy

The Union of Soviet Socialist Republics (USSR, 1922-1991) profoundly shaped this region's geography, economy, and politics. Understanding the Soviet legacy is essential for comprehending current conditions.

Formation and Expansion

The Soviet Union emerged from the 1917 Russian Revolution and eventually incorporated 15 republics. Central planning replaced market economics, and collectivized agriculture replaced private farming.

Industrialization and Urbanization

The Soviets rapidly industrialized, often relocating factories to Siberia and establishing new cities. The Trans-Siberian Railway connected Moscow to the Pacific. Population was redistributed to develop resource frontiers.

Collapse and Aftermath

The Soviet Union dissolved in 1991, creating 15 independent states. The transition brought economic disruption, population decline, and political instability. Russia emerged as the dominant successor state, inheriting the USSR's nuclear arsenal and UN Security Council seat.

üåê Global Theme: Energy Geopolitics

Russia holds vast oil and natural gas reserves, making energy a key tool of geopolitical influence. Russia ranks among the world's top three producers of both oil and natural gas.

Energy as Power

European dependence on Russian gas gave Moscow significant leverage. Pipelines like Nord Stream connected Russia directly to Western Europe, bypassing transit countries like Ukraine. Energy revenues funded Russian government spending and military modernization.

The Ukraine Conflict

The 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine marked a dramatic escalation in post-Soviet tensions. Geographic factors include:

  • Ukraine's position: A buffer state between Russia and NATO
  • Crimea: Annexed by Russia in 2014; home to strategic naval base at Sevastopol
  • Donbas: Industrial region with Russian-speaking population
  • Energy transit: Pipelines crossing Ukraine to Europe

The war has reshaped global energy markets, intensified NATO expansion, and created Europe's largest refugee crisis since World War II.

üèîÔ∏è Central Asia: The Post-Soviet Stans

The five Central Asian republics share Soviet heritage, predominantly Muslim populations, and authoritarian governance. Key features:

  • Kazakhstan: Largest, with significant oil reserves and Russian minority
  • Uzbekistan: Most populous, major cotton producer
  • Turkmenistan: Enormous gas reserves, highly isolated
  • Kyrgyzstan: Mountainous, most democratic
  • Tajikistan: Poorest, experienced civil war in 1990s

Texas and Russia both rank among the world's top oil and gas producers. As sanctions limited Russian exports, Texas LNG exports to Europe increased significantly.

Russification

Near Abroad
The 14 former Soviet republics that Russia views as its essential sphere of influence.
Oligarchs
Ultra-wealthy business leaders who acquired state assets during privatization and hold political influence.
